(Who we tell people we are)

I was born in Omaha, but I'm from a small town called Valentine, Nebraska.
I've lived in Valentine my entire life.

It was July 3, 2002.
I was fourteen years old.

My dad brought home a computer from work.
It was an IBM desktop from 1999.

He plugged in the computer and it beeped.
The air filled with the hum of fans and the smell of dust.

I watched him set it up for hours on end.
I didn't understand anything he typed into the terminal.

By the time August rolled around, I knew how to use it on my own.
I loved that machine, and I loved what I could make it do.

One day in early October, there was a van parked in front of my house.
It was the first thing I saw when I got home from school.

My computer had incredible new abilities.
Therefore, so did I.

I could communicate with anyone else on Earth, no matter how far away.
Provided they had a computer, of course.

I met so many new, unique people, and learned so many incredible things.
And then I encountered an infohazard: a piece of information that worked its way into my brain.

I had always been uncomfortable in my own skin, that's why the computer was incredible.
It extended my body beyond the confines of my skin and gave me a safe place to go.

This infohazard taught me that my body wasn't as static as people taught me.
Not only could I change the part of my body outside my skin, I could change the part inside as well.

I wanted to peel back the membranes that enclosed me and embrace my new identity.
My new identity as multiplicitous, cybernetic, and non-individual.

My new identity as a woman.
